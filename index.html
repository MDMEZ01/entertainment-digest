FCC to Vote August 6 on Eliminating 39% National Broadcast Ownership Cap — Biggest Media Deregulation in Decades

  • FCC Chairman Brendan Carr announced Wednesday that the Commission will vote August 6 to repeal its national television ownership rule, which prohibits a single company from reaching more than 39% of U.S. TV households; the rule would be replaced with case-by-case public interest review
    • Republican-controlled 2-1 FCC makes the vote a near certainty; Democratic Commissioner Anna Gomez called it an “unlawful effort to hand control of the public airwaves to billionaire buddies of this administration”; the move is widely expected to face immediate legal challenge
    • Beneficiaries of repeal include Sinclair Broadcast Group, Nexstar Media Group, and Fox Corporation — all of which have been publicly aligned with the Trump administration and lobbied aggressively for the cap’s removal for years
  • The timing is significant: with WarnerMount (CBS/CNN) seeking regulatory approval and Fox–Roku under HSR review, the FCC vote would dramatically alter the landscape for any broadcast-oriented consolidation; a Sinclair megadeal could be feasible for the first time
  • Dylan Byers (Puck) notes the move is part of a broader deregulatory sweep — the same environment that cleared WarnerMount at the federal level while state AGs scramble to fill the gap; the August 6 vote date is deliberate, landing just before the EU AI Act enforcement date (August 2) and potentially the WarnerMount TRO window

📈 Today’s Deep Dive

The FCC’s August 6 Vote Could Redraw the Entire U.S. Media Map — and No One Is Talking About It

Why It Matters
  • While everyone watches the WarnerMount TRO drama, the FCC is moving to eliminate the single rule that has governed American broadcast ownership since the Reagan era: the 39% national household cap that prohibits any company from owning TV stations reaching more than 39% of U.S. households; its removal via case-by-case “public interest review” is the most significant deregulatory action in U.S. media since the 1996 Telecom Act — and it arrives with almost none of the press attention of the WarnerMount merger
  • The cap’s elimination would, for the first time, make it legal for a single company to own TV stations reaching 100% of U.S. households; Sinclair (currently at roughly 35% via its recent acquisitions), Nexstar (33%), and Fox Owned Stations (29%) would each be free to pursue transformative consolidation; a Sinclair-Nexstar combination — previously unthinkable — would create a local news behemoth reaching virtually every American market
  • The FCC move is not occurring in a vacuum: it’s part of the same deregulatory environment that cleared WarnerMount at the federal level, blessed Fox–Roku, and is watching as state AGs scramble to fill the regulatory vacuum the Trump FCC refuses to occupy; the common thread is the deliberate dismantling of the oversight architecture that governed U.S. media for 50 years
The Money
  • Local television stations remain among the most profitable assets in media — high political advertising revenues every two years, sticky local news audiences, and real estate-grade cash flows; the ability to own unlimited stations unlocks billions in private equity acquisition capacity; Blackstone, KKR, and Apollo have all signaled interest in broadcast consolidation if the regulatory ceiling is removed
  • For WarnerMount specifically: CBS Stations (the largest local TV group in America, reaching 74% of U.S. households via CBS affiliates) was previously subject to the cap in combination with Paramount’s existing CBS holdings; if the cap is gone by October — when WarnerMount hopes to close — the antitrust math for the deal’s broadcast component changes entirely
Historical Context
  • The 39% cap was established in 2004 as a compromise after the FCC’s 2003 attempt to raise it to 45% was blocked by Congress in a rare bipartisan revolt; the cap has survived multiple court challenges; this is the first time a ruling-party FCC has moved to simply eliminate it rather than raise it — a distinction that could make judicial review sharper, since “raise the number” is arguable but “eliminate the number entirely” is the kind of administrative overreach that courts have recently scrutinized
Watch For
  • August 6 FCC vote: Democratic Commissioner Gomez is expected to issue a lengthy dissent that becomes the roadmap for legal challenges; look for whether public interest groups file for a stay before the rule takes effect
  • Whether Sinclair or Nexstar file any ownership transactions in the week after the vote passes; the first acquisition under the new rule will set the deal price and the litigation template simultaneously
  • Congressional reaction: the 2003 bipartisan revolt against a far more modest cap increase suggests there are still votes to override; watch whether Senate Republicans from small-market states — where local news monopolies most directly affect constituents — break ranks
