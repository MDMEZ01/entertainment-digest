WarnerMount’s Nuclear Option: Why Paramount’s Team Is Already Thinking About SCOTUS — and Why That’s the Point

Why It Matters
  • Eriq Gardner’s Puck analysis reveals that Paramount’s strategy in the WarnerMount antitrust fight may extend far beyond winning the injunction — chief legal officer Makan Delrahim publicly floated the idea that 7–9 Supreme Court justices might vote to revisit Philadelphia National Bank (1963), the precedent underlying all modern merger concentration law; the Supreme Court gambit is both a genuine legal strategy and a negotiating signal: the cost of a prolonged fight could be that antitrust law itself gets rewritten
  • States know this: their complaint was deliberately “old-school” — HHI calculations, narrow market definitions, no streaming, no labor markets — because it is built to survive judicial scrutiny, not generate press; AG Bonta retained Richard Parker (former FTC Bureau director) as outside counsel specifically because Parker knows how to write complaints that don’t get dismissed on procedural grounds
  • The structural fight is between two visions of what the media market is: Bonta’s team says it’s theatrical films and cable channels; Ellison’s team says it’s the entire integrated ecosystem of tech-driven global content distribution, where Netflix, Amazon, and Apple are the real competition
The Money
  • $650M in quarterly ticking fees begin accruing October 1 if the deal isn’t closed; states argue Paramount voluntarily negotiated that provision and cannot weaponize it against judicial review; a TRO would likely preserve the status quo through at least September; Semafor reported taxpayers could be on the hook for $600M–$1.2B if a wrongly issued injunction delayed the deal
  • The combined company would carry $78B in debt; Zaslav’s WBD merger aftermath — Batgirl shelved, mass layoffs, write-downs — is the states’ most powerful evidentiary exhibit for what happens when two over-leveraged studios merge

  • The last time states successfully blocked a major media merger against federal clearance was AT&T/Time Warner (2019, ultimately permitted) — but the framing here is closer to the Penguin Random House/Simon & Schuster block (2022), which was the DOJ blocking on narrow market definitions that the court accepted; Bonta is betting the same playbook works in film

  • Whether Paul Clement enters a formal appearance in the Ninth Circuit: if Paramount’s top SCOTUS advocate is actively briefing at the appellate level from day one, the SCOTUS gambit is a real strategy, not a negotiating bluff; and whether David Ellison makes any move toward Sacramento or Washington in the next 10 days to try to resolve the case before it becomes a genuine constitutional moment
